What is a Gele?

The gele is more than a headwrap. It is language. It is memory. It is the silent crown a woman wears to declare presence, ancestry and joy.

Worn across West Africa — most famously among the Yoruba of Nigeria — the gele transforms ordinary fabric into sculptural celebration. Its folds, fans and rosettes are pleated into being by hand, often by elders who learned from their mothers, who learned from theirs.

Origins & Etymology

The gele — pronounced gay-lay — is the Yoruba word for a woman's headwrap, and the form most people now mean when they say "Nigerian headwrap." The word is old; the cloth and shape are older. In the photographic record alone, sculptural geles appear in Lagos studio portraits from the 1880s, in the same posed grammar that the form still uses today: bride seated, mother standing, light falling across the architecture of the cloth.

Anthropologists trace the wider African headwrap back several centuries earlier — to the cloth headcoverings of the medieval Mali and Songhai empires, and to the indigo wraps of the Yoruba kingdoms before contact with Europe. What is distinctive about the modern gele is not the practice of covering the head — that is universal across West Africa — but the sculptural, fan-shaped, deliberately public form the Yoruba refined into a ceremonial object. By the early twentieth century, the tall pleated gele was already a Lagos signature, and by the post-independence years (1960s onwards) it had been adopted, with regional inflections, by Nigerian women of every ethnicity.

The form itself is unusual in textile history because it is held entirely by the cloth's own stiffness. There are no pins, no glue, no sewing. A skilled stylist can tie a tall sculptural gele in seven minutes; a learner can take an hour. The stiffness comes from the weave — aso-oke is naturally rigid; brocade is stiffened with a starching agent during finishing — and the architecture comes from the hands.

The Cloths That Make a Gele

A gele is only as good as the cloth it is tied from. The five cloths below are the working palette of the modern Nigerian wedding stylist. They are not interchangeable: each has a season, a region and a register.

Aso-oke

The classical Yoruba cloth, hand-woven on narrow looms in strips of about four inches and joined into wider lengths. There are three traditional types — alaari (a deep wine-red, dyed with kola or imported alizarin), sanyan (a soft beige, woven from wild Anaphe moth silk), and etu (deep indigo with fine cream stripes). A bride who wears aso-oke for her traditional wedding is invoking the oldest layer of Yoruba ceremonial dress; the gele cut from the same cloth as her iro and buba is one of the most legible visual signals at any Nigerian wedding. Read more in "The Aso-Oke Arrived" — an essay on the day the cloth comes home from the weaver.

Sego (Brocade)

Sego is the Yoruba name for the stiff, embossed brocade — most often imported from Switzerland and Austria — that became the dominant gele cloth from the 1980s onwards. It is engineered for sculpture: heavy, slightly waxy to the touch, and almost impossible to wear without it remembering the fold you put in last. The very tall fan-shaped geles seen on Bella Naija Weddings and at every Lagos reception in the last twenty years are almost always sego. It is the cloth of the modern white wedding.

Damask

A patterned woven cloth, lighter than sego but heavier than ordinary cotton, often imported from Europe. Damask sits between aso-oke and brocade in stiffness and is a popular choice for the introduction ceremony and the second outfit at a traditional wedding — softer than sego, easier on the skin, more forgiving for first-time wearers.

Velvet

Velvet gele is a contemporary form — most associated with the Lagos couture wave of the 2010s. Velvet is too soft to hold a tall fan, so velvet geles are tied in low, plush, often turban-like shapes that frame the face. Pairs beautifully with heavy gold jewellery; reads richer under low light, which is why velvet is often the choice for evening receptions.

Lace & jacquard

Lace gele is more recent still. The cloth is too unstructured to hold its own shape, so lace geles are tied over a stiffer underlay (often a folded square of brocade) and are worn most often at the white wedding rather than the traditional. Jacquard sits between damask and brocade and is a favourite of the asoebi gele — the matching cloth tied by the bride's friends and family.

The Tying — Five Classical Shapes

Modern stylists work in dozens of variations, but every variation descends from one of five classical shapes. Each shape has a face it suits, an occasion it belongs to, and a register — quieter or louder — within Nigerian formal dress.

1. The classic fan

The shape most non-Nigerians picture when they hear "gele" — a tall, pleated fan that rises from the centre of the head and spreads behind the ears. Suits oval and heart-shaped faces; reads loudest at the white wedding. The fan is the bride's calling card.

2. The infinity (or shaped tie)

A lower, sculpted shape that loops around the crown like a figure-of-eight, often with a soft rosette to one side. Suits round and square faces; reads dressed but not commanding. The infinity is the choice of the mother of the bride, the older auntie, the woman who has been to enough weddings to know that the fan is for the bride.

3. The rosette

A central spiral fold, usually low to the head, that finishes in a flat rose-shaped knot at the side. Reads softer and more architectural at once; popular for the introduction ceremony, where the bride's family wear it together.

4. The avant-garde

The umbrella term for any gele that breaks the classical rules — asymmetrical fans, double crowns, geles wrapped to one side and pinned with feathers or fresh flowers. Reads editorial; popular on the runway and on Instagram. Brides who choose avant-garde for the white wedding usually keep a classical fan ready for the family photographs.

5. The turban (velvet or soft-cloth)

A low, wrapped shape with the cloth tucked into itself rather than folded into pleats. Suits oblong and diamond faces; reads modern and intimate. The turban is the gele of the second outfit, the after-party, the second-day brunch.

When the Gele Is Worn

The Nigerian formal calendar gives the gele several stages, each with its own register. A bride may go through three or four geles in a single weekend, a guest one or two.

The introduction

The first formal meeting of the two families. The bride wears a softer cloth — damask or aso-oke — tied in a rosette or low fan. The introduction is the dress rehearsal, and the gele is dressed accordingly: present but not commanding.

The traditional wedding

The fullest expression of regional dress. A Yoruba bride wears aso-oke, with the gele tied to match the iro and buba. An Igbo bride wears coral and lace with an ichafu (the Igbo headwrap). A Hausa bride wears a gyale, the long veil-like wrap that frames the face. The traditional is the gele's classical home.

The white wedding

The Christian or church ceremony, imported and adapted. The bride often goes Western — white dress, no gele — and changes into a gele for the reception, which has become its own genre. The post-vow gele is a brocade fan, tall and decisive, paired with the change-of-outfit aso-ebi cloth that the bridal party wears in matched colour.

For the small public moments inside this ceremony, see our essays on the first look, the crying groom, and the pre-wedding shoot.

The civil ceremony

For the diaspora — couples in Houston, Manchester, Dublin — the civil ceremony has quietly become the main event. The gele is dressed up accordingly. See "The Civil Ceremony Loosens Its Tie" for an essay on the courthouse Nigerian wedding.

Funerals and remembrance ceremonies

Black, white or family-colour aso-ebi, tied in a low fan or infinity. The gele at a Nigerian funeral is a public statement of relation: who tied it, what cloth they tied it from, and where they stand in the grieving family.

Naming ceremonies, thanksgivings, chieftaincies

Any moment where the family gathers in matched cloth, the gele is the headpiece. The cloth is chosen first, the gele follows.

Regional Variations

The gele is most often coded as Yoruba, but Nigerian women across ethnicities have their own equivalent forms. The four below are the most frequently photographed.

Yoruba (south-western Nigeria)

The classical gele home. Aso-oke and sego dominate; the fan and rosette are the working shapes. A Yoruba bride at a traditional wedding will almost always wear aso-oke; at the white wedding, sego. See "Cultural Elegance Meets Electric Chemistry" on the Yoruba bridal grammar.

Igbo (south-eastern Nigeria)

The Igbo equivalent is ichafu, a softer headwrap closer in form to the Yoruba gele than to a turban. Igbo brides traditionally wear coral beads, a lace blouse and wrapper, and an ichafu in a low rosette or tied with a soft fan. The aesthetic register is jewelled rather than sculptural.

Hausa (northern Nigeria)

The Hausa equivalent is the gyale, a longer, lighter wrap that drapes from the head down across the shoulders, framing the face. Hausa brides often wear a gyale rather than a fan-shaped gele, paired with intricate lace and gold jewellery. Read "The Bride and the Sultan" for a portrait of a northern Nigerian wedding.

Edo, Itsekiri, Ijaw & the Niger Delta

The southern coastal peoples have their own headwrap traditions — often closer in form to the Yoruba gele than the Hausa gyale, but with distinct cloths (Edo coral, Itsekiri damask in family colours) and tying conventions. Cross-pollination with the Yoruba tradition is heavy; many southern Nigerian brides today choose between regional dress and a 'pan-Nigerian' bridal gele.

Care, Storage & Travel

A well-made gele lasts decades. The cloth your grandmother wore at her wedding can be untied, cleaned and re-tied for yours. Three notes from the atelier:

  • Storage. After wearing, gently unfold the gele, brush it down for pins, and store it flat or loosely rolled in a cotton bag. Avoid hanging — gravity will deform the weave over time.
  • Cleaning. Aso-oke can be hand-washed cold and air-dried in shade. Brocade should be dry-cleaned only; the stiffening agent in sego is water-soluble and a wash will leave the cloth limp. Velvet should be steamed, not ironed.
  • Travel. If you are flying with a gele for a wedding abroad, fold it once, lay it flat in your suitcase between two layers of tissue paper, and tie it on arrival. Pre-tied geles do not survive baggage handling.

For a small piece of cloth that holds its shape by hand, the gele is remarkably durable. Most family pieces in Lagos are older than the women wearing them.

What Does a Gele Cost?

The most asked question, and the most context-dependent. A gele's price is the cloth plus the tying — and either can range from modest to extraordinary.

Cloth

  • Aso-oke — A traditional aso-oke gele cloth, hand-woven by Yoruba weavers in Iseyin or Oyo, ranges from ₦25,000 to ₦150,000 (roughly £14–£80 / $18–$100) for a standard two-yard length. Bespoke bridal aso-oke woven in a family colour and stripe can run substantially higher — ₦300,000+ for the full bridal set of iro, buba, gele and ipele.
  • Sego (brocade) — Imported Swiss or Austrian brocade ranges from ₦15,000 to ₦60,000 (£8–£32) per piece, depending on the weight, finish and embossing pattern. Heavier, gold-shot brocades for the white wedding sit at the top of this range.
  • Damask & jacquard — Lighter, more affordable: ₦8,000 to ₦25,000 (£4–£14) per piece. The everyday choice for asoebi (matched group cloth).
  • Velvet — Couture velvet gele cloth runs ₦20,000 to ₦80,000 (£10–£42), with the highest prices for embroidered or hand-beaded pieces.
  • Lace — Lace headpieces are usually sold as part of a fuller bridal lace set; the gele portion ranges from ₦10,000 upwards.

Tying

A professional gele stylist in Lagos charges between ₦5,000 and ₦40,000 (£3–£22) to tie a single gele on the morning of a wedding, depending on her reputation and the complexity of the shape. Top stylists — those whose work appears regularly on Bella Naija Weddings — charge ₦60,000+ for the bride and additional fees for the bridesmaids and the mothers.

Pre-tied geles

For brides and guests who do not have access to a stylist — or for international shipping — pre-tied geles are sewn into shape and worn like a fitted hat. A high-quality pre-tied brocade gele runs £25–£60 in the UK and US diaspora markets; a custom pre-tied aso-oke piece, considerably more.

FREQUENTLY ASKED

Common Questions.

The questions our readers and customers ask most often. If yours isn't here, write to us.

What is a gele?

A gele is a Nigerian headwrap, most associated with Yoruba women, sculpted by hand from a stiff length of fabric — usually aso-oke, brocade (sego), or damask. It is worn at weddings, naming ceremonies, funerals and other formal occasions, and signals presence, ancestry and joy.

What is the difference between a gele and a regular headwrap?

A gele is sculpted, structured and architectural — folded into fans, rosettes or tall crowns — and held by the cloth's own stiffness rather than pins or sewing. A regular headwrap is softer, casual, and worn for everyday styling rather than ceremony.

What is aso-oke?

Aso-oke is a hand-loomed cloth woven by the Yoruba in narrow strips and joined together. It comes in three classical types — alaari (deep red), sanyan (beige, from wild silk) and etu (indigo). Aso-oke is the traditional cloth for both gele and the bridal iro and buba; it is dense, slightly textured, and holds a fold beautifully. See our section on cloths for more.

What is sego (brocade)?

Sego is the Yoruba name for a stiff, embossed brocade — usually imported from Switzerland or Austria — that became the most common gele cloth from the 1980s onwards. Its stiffness allows for the very tall, fan-shaped geles seen at modern Lagos weddings.

How long is a piece of gele cloth?

A standard gele cloth is around two yards (approximately 1.8 metres) long and 24 inches wide. Aso-oke geles are typically two narrow strips joined together; brocade and damask geles are sold pre-cut to the standard length.

Who ties the gele at a Nigerian wedding?

Most brides hire a professional gele stylist (sometimes called an "auntie" even when she is a peer) on the morning of the wedding. Family members are often tied by the same stylist or by older female relatives who learned the form from their own mothers.

When is a gele worn?

At weddings (the introduction, traditional and white wedding), at burial and remembrance ceremonies, at naming ceremonies, at chieftaincy installations, at religious thanksgivings, and at any formal occasion where aso-ebi (matching family or group cloth) is being worn. See our occasions section.

Do Igbo and Hausa women wear gele?

Yes — though the form varies. Igbo women wear ichafu, a softer headwrap that is closer in shape to the Yoruba gele than to a turban; Hausa women wear gyale, a longer veil-like wrap that frames the face and shoulders. The sculptural fan-shaped gele most associated with Lagos is Yoruba in origin but is now worn by Nigerian women of all ethnicities. More in regional variations.

How do I store a gele?

After wearing, gently unfold the gele, brush it down to remove pins or hair, and store it flat or loosely rolled in a cotton bag. Aso-oke can be ironed on low heat through a cotton cloth; brocade should not be ironed at high temperatures, as the stiffening agent can melt or scorch.

Is the gele only for women?

Traditionally, yes — the gele is a feminine form. The Yoruba men's equivalent is the fila, a soft cap (most often the agbada-paired aso-oke fila or the velvet fila in alaari red). At a Yoruba wedding, the bride wears gele and the groom wears fila in the same family colour.
